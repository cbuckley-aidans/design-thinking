DESIGN CRITERIA

Design criteria are the rules that help you check if your design is good. Think of them as a checklist for your project. Try to create 4-6 criteria that you can test at the end of your project.

Often, your client or teacher will tell you what they need. You can turn these needs into clear design criteria.

CREATING GOOD CRITERIA

1

BE SPECIFIC

Use clear numbers instead of vague words. This makes it easier to tell if you've met your goal.

❌ Too vague: "The water bottle must be light"
✓ Better: "The water bottle must weigh less than 500g"
2

MAKE IT TESTABLE

You need to be able to check if your design meets each criterion. There should be a clear way to test it.

❌ Hard to test: "Must be strong"
✓ Better: "Must survive a 1m drop test without breaking"
3

KEEP IT ACHIEVABLE

Make sure you can actually meet your criteria with the materials, time, and skills you have.

❌ Too difficult: "Made from titanium"
✓ Better: "Made from recycled plastic"
4

MAKE IT RELEVANT

Each criterion should solve a real problem for your users. Don't include features nobody needs.

❌ Not relevant: "Beach toy must be fireproof"
✓ Better: "Beach toy must float in water"

USING DESIGN PRINCIPLES TO CREATE CRITERIA

Dieter Rams, a famous industrial designer, created 10 principles that define good design. You can use these principles to think of relevant criteria for your own projects.

Below, each principle is explained with questions you can ask yourself to create specific design criteria.

GOOD DESIGN IS INNOVATIVE

Your design should offer something new or solve a problem in a fresh way.

Ask yourself:

  • • What new feature can my design include that others don't have?
  • • How can I solve this problem differently than existing products?
  • • What would make users say "I wish I'd thought of that!"?

Example Criterion: "My water bottle design must include a feature that no existing bottles in our classroom have (like a built-in fruit infuser or collapsible design)."

GOOD DESIGN MAKES A PRODUCT USEFUL

Every part of your design should have a clear purpose that helps users achieve their goals.

Ask yourself:

  • • What specific tasks must my design help users complete?
  • • Which features are absolutely necessary vs. just nice to have?
  • • How will I know if my design actually makes life easier for users?

Example Criterion: "My homework planner must allow students to record and find any assignment in under 30 seconds during user testing."

GOOD DESIGN IS AESTHETIC

Your design should be visually pleasing and make users feel good about using it.

Ask yourself:

  • • What colours, shapes, or textures would appeal to my target users?
  • • How can I make my design look professional and well-thought-out?
  • • What visual elements will make users want to show off my design?

Example Criterion: "My phone case design must receive positive aesthetic ratings from at least 8 out of 10 students in my target age group."

GOOD DESIGN MAKES A PRODUCT UNDERSTANDABLE

Users should immediately understand how to use your design without needing instructions.

Ask yourself:

  • • Can someone figure out how to use my design just by looking at it?
  • • What visual cues or labels might help users understand the purpose?
  • • How can I test if my design is intuitive to use?

Example Criterion: "New users must be able to use my app interface correctly within 2 minutes without any instructions or help."

GOOD DESIGN IS UNOBTRUSIVE

Your design should blend seamlessly into users' lives without drawing unnecessary attention to itself.

Ask yourself:

  • • Does my design fit naturally into the user's environment?
  • • Are there any features that might distract from the main purpose?
  • • How can I make my design feel like a natural part of daily life?

Example Criterion: "My desk organizer must fit seamlessly into existing classroom setups without requiring users to rearrange their workspace."

GOOD DESIGN IS HONEST

Your design should clearly communicate what it does without misleading users about its capabilities.

Ask yourself:

  • • Does my design accurately represent what it can actually do?
  • • Are there any features that might give users false expectations?
  • • How can I ensure users understand the real benefits and limitations?

Example Criterion: "My study timer app must accurately track time to within 5 seconds over a 1-hour period and clearly display actual functionality."

GOOD DESIGN IS LONG-LASTING

Your design should remain useful and relevant over time, not quickly become outdated.

Ask yourself:

  • • Will users still find my design valuable next year? In 5 years?
  • • What materials or design choices will help it last physically?
  • • How can I avoid trends that might make it look dated quickly?

Example Criterion: "My backpack design must withstand daily use for a full school year (tested through 180 days of simulated wear)."

GOOD DESIGN IS THOROUGH DOWN TO THE LAST DETAIL

Every aspect of your design should be carefully considered, even the smallest details.

Ask yourself:

  • • What small details might users notice and appreciate?
  • • Are there any rough edges or unfinished aspects to address?
  • • How can I ensure quality in every component of my design?

Example Criterion: "My product prototype must have smooth, finished edges with no visible glue marks or rough surfaces when examined closely."

GOOD DESIGN IS ENVIRONMENTALLY FRIENDLY

Your design should minimise environmental impact throughout its lifecycle.

Ask yourself:

  • • What materials can I use that are recycled or recyclable?
  • • How can I reduce waste in my manufacturing process?
  • • Can my design help users live more sustainably?

Example Criterion: "My lunch container must be made from at least 75% recycled materials and be completely recyclable at end of life."

GOOD DESIGN IS AS LITTLE DESIGN AS POSSIBLE

Focus only on what's essential. Remove anything that doesn't add real value for users.

Ask yourself:

  • • Which features are truly necessary vs. just decorative?
  • • What can I remove without losing functionality?
  • • How can I achieve maximum impact with minimum complexity?

Example Criterion: "My design must accomplish its main function using no more than 3 moving parts and 2 different materials."

TRY IT YOURSELF

Pick your design project and work through these steps:

Step 1: Choose 3-5 principles

Select the principles most relevant to your project. You don't need to use all 10.

Step 2: Ask the questions

For each chosen principle, answer the questions provided above.

Step 3: Write specific criteria

Turn your answers into measurable design criteria using numbers, tests, or clear benchmarks.

Step 4: Check if you can test them

Make sure each criterion can be tested with the resources you have available.

USER STORIES

User stories help you understand exactly what your users need. They are short, simple statements that describe what a user wants to do and why they want to do it.

A good user story focuses on the user's goal, not the specific features they might need.

HOW TO WRITE USER STORIES

THE USER STORY FORMAT

As a type of user, I want to do something so that I get this benefit.

WHO?

The type of person who will use your design. Be specific about who they are.

WHAT?

The action they want to take. Focus on what they're trying to do, not how.

WHY?

The benefit they get. This explains why the action matters to them.

Example: As a student with a heavy backpack, I want to easily access my water bottle without removing my backpack so that I can stay hydrated between classes without being late.

1

IDENTIFY YOUR USERS

Think about different types of people who will use your design. Be specific about who they are and what makes them unique.

Example users: Left-handed students, athletes carrying sports equipment, teachers who move between classrooms
2

UNDERSTAND THEIR GOALS

What is each user trying to achieve? Focus on their goals, not the specific features they might want.

Example goal: Instead of "wants a cup holder," think "wants to keep drink from spilling while walking"
3

CONSIDER THE BENEFITS

Why does this goal matter to them? What problem does it solve or what benefit does it provide?

Example benefit: "So I don't have to stop and set down my belongings every time I need a drink"
4

WRITE MULTIPLE STORIES

Create several user stories for different types of users. This helps you see your design from different perspectives.

Tip: Aim for 5-10 different user stories that cover various users and scenarios

TIPS FOR GREAT USER STORIES

Keep stories simple

Each story should focus on one goal. If it's too complex, break it into smaller stories.

Use real people as inspiration

Think about actual people you know who might use your design.

Focus on problems, not solutions

Describe what the user needs to achieve, not how your design will work.

Be specific about the user

"Student" is too general. "7th grade student who plays sports" is better.

REMEMBER

  • User stories help you understand what people really need
  • Follow the format: As a (who), I want to (what) so that (why)
  • Focus on the user's goal, not specific features
  • Create multiple stories for different types of users

EXAMPLE CRITERIA & STORIES

Below are examples of good design criteria and user stories that you can use as models for your own project.

DESIGN CRITERIA EXAMPLES

1

WATER BOTTLE DESIGN

Description

My water bottle design will be innovative. It will fit in a standard car cup holder while also being combinable to create a larger thermos.

Measure

I will know I've met this goal when my bottle fits in all standard cup holders (7-9 cm diameter) and I can attach two bottles together without leaking.

2

BACKPACK ORGANISER

Description

My backpack organiser will keep school supplies neat. It will have spaces for at least 5 pens/pencils, a calculator, and 3 small notebooks.

Measure

The design succeeds if a student can find and remove any item in less than 5 seconds during a timed test with 5 different students.

USER STORY EXAMPLES

1

LOCKER ORGANISER

Story 1: As a student who plays sports, I want to keep my gym clothes separate from my school books so that my books don't get smelly or damp.

Story 2: As a student with many textbooks, I want to quickly find the right book between classes so that I'm not late for my next class.

Story 3: As a student who shares a locker, I want to clearly see which items are mine so that I don't accidentally take my locker partner's stuff.

2

HOMEWORK PLANNER

Story 1: As a student with ADHD, I want to break down big assignments into smaller steps so that I don't feel overwhelmed and can track my progress.

Story 2: As a busy student with after-school activities, I want to see all my upcoming deadlines in one place so that I can plan my time and not miss anything important.

Story 3: As a parent checking homework, I want to easily see what assignments are due this week so that I can help my child stay on track.

PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER

LUNCH BOX REDESIGN PROJECT

User Stories:

As a student who walks to school, I want to carry my lunch without it spilling in my backpack so that my books and homework stay clean.

As a student who enjoys hot lunches, I want to keep my food warm until lunchtime so that I can enjoy a hot meal without using a microwave.

Design Criteria:

  1. Leak-proof: The lunchbox must not leak when shaken upside down for 10 seconds.
  2. Insulated: Must keep food at least 40°F (22°C) above room temperature for 4 hours.
  3. Lightweight: Must weigh less than 500g when empty.
  4. Durable: Must withstand being dropped from desk height (75cm) 10 times without cracking.

KEY TAKEAWAYS

  • Start with user stories to understand what people need
  • Create specific, measurable criteria based on those needs
  • Make sure you can actually test if your design meets each criterion
  • Keep your users' real problems at the center of your design
