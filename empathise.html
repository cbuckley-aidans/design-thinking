WHAT IS EMPATHY?

Empathy means understanding how other people feel and what they need. As designers, we need to put ourselves in the users' shoes to make things they'll love to use.

When we have empathy for users, we can:

  • Understand what users really need (not just what they say they need)
  • Find out what makes users frustrated or confused
  • Learn what would make users happy with our design
  • Create designs that people actually want to use

WHY DESIGNERS NEED EMPATHY

AVOID PROBLEMS

Without empathy, we might make things that look cool but are hard to use. This wastes time and money, and frustrates users.

BUILD TRUST

When users feel understood, they trust your product more. They're more likely to keep using it and tell others about it.

FIND NEW IDEAS

Watching and talking to users can spark new ideas for features or improvements you hadn't thought of before.

INCLUDE EVERYONE

Empathy helps us design for all users, including those with different abilities, backgrounds, and needs than our own.

HOW TO PRACTICE EMPATHY

There are three main ways to develop empathy for your users:

ROLEPLAY

Watch users as they use a product. See where they get stuck or confused. Notice what they do differently than you expected.

INTERVIEW

Ask users questions about their needs, goals, and frustrations. Listen carefully to understand what they really want and why.

RESEARCH

Find information about users' needs online. Use search engines, videos, and other online resources to discover how people solve similar problems.

REMEMBER

Good design isn't about what you think is cool or clever. It's about making something that works well for the people who will use it.

CLIENT ROLEPLAYING

Watch people use their current tools to spot problems they might not tell you about. This helps you understand how people really use products in their daily life.

1

USE REAL TASKS

Ask people to show you how they do actual work with their current tools - not made-up scenarios.

Example: "Show me how you organise your locker from start to finish."
2

ASK THEM TO TALK

Ask the person to say their thoughts out loud as they work. This helps you understand what they're thinking.

Example: "Please tell me what you're thinking as you work."
3

WATCH FOR PAUSES

Pay attention when the person stops or looks confused - these show where they might be having problems.

Example: Notice sighs, frowns, or when they say "This part is annoying."
4

SPOT WORKAROUNDS

Look for clever solutions the person has made to deal with problems in their current tools.

Example: They might organize items in their locker by colour or size when the space isn't designed well.
5

NOTE THEIR WORDS

Write down the exact words people use to describe features - use these same words in your design.

Example: If they call it a "dashboard" instead of "home page," use their term.

HELPFUL QUESTIONS

"What are you trying to do here?"

Helps you understand their goal

"Why did you choose that option?"

Shows how they make decisions

"Is this how you normally do this?"

Reveals regular habits vs. one-time actions

"I saw you pause - was something unclear?"

Finds moments of confusion

MAIN POINTS

  • Watch real tasks, not fake ones
  • Pay attention when people pause or look confused
  • Clever workarounds show where tools need to be better
  • Use the same words in your design that users use

TALKING WITH USERS

Good questions help you learn what users really need. Interviews help you understand the goals and frustrations that watching alone might miss.

1

ASK OPEN QUESTIONS

Don't ask: "Do you like this?"

This only gets a yes or no answer

Do ask: "How do you feel about this?"

This gets a more detailed answer

Good questions start with:

How... What... Why... Tell me about...
2

FIND PAIN POINTS

Ask: "What frustrates you most when using this?"

Helps you find what needs fixing

Shows what's most important to fix first

Finds clever fixes users have made

3

LEARN ABOUT WHEN AND WHERE

Ask: "When and where do you use this most?"

Where they use it

When they use it

How it fits with other tools

Think about: What device they use, time of day, location, noise level, distractions

4

FIND OUT WISHES

Ask: "What would make this perfect for you?"

Reveals features they want

Shows what they hope for

Gives ideas for improvements

Follow up with: "Why would that be perfect?" to understand deeper needs

EXAMPLE QUESTIONS

ABOUT GOALS

  • "What are you trying to achieve when you use this?"
  • "How do you know when you've been successful?"

ABOUT CHALLENGES

  • "What parts of this take you the most time?"
  • "When was the last time you felt stuck using this?"

ABOUT FEELINGS

  • "How do you feel when you use this feature?"
  • "What part of this do you enjoy the most?"

ABOUT IMPROVEMENTS

  • "If you had a magic wand, what would you change?"
  • "What's one thing that would make this easier to use?"

MAIN POINTS

  • Ask questions that start with how, what, or why
  • Find out what frustrates users the most
  • Understand when and where people use your design
  • Ask what would make the design perfect for them

ONLINE RESEARCH

Looking at what's already online can teach you a lot about your users. You can find out what people like, what problems they have, and what solutions already exist.

1

CHECK REVIEWS

Look at reviews of similar products to see what people like and don't like.

Example: Read reviews of locker organizers to learn what features people want.
2

SEE VISUALS AND EXAMPLES

Look at images and examples of how people organize their spaces in the real world.

Example: Look at Pinterest boards of "school locker organization" to see creative solutions.
3

USE ONLINE RESOURCES

Search engines, YouTube videos, blogs, and research tools can help you find useful information quickly.

Example: Watch YouTube videos of "dorm room organization" to see real-life solutions.
4

FOLLOW TRENDS

Look for popular trends to understand what people currently care about.

Example: If sustainable products are trending, users might want eco-friendly options.
5

SEARCH SMART

Learn to search in ways that find exactly what you need.

Example: Instead of "locker ideas," try "high school locker organization solutions for small spaces."

SEARCH TIPS

Use quote marks

"locker organization ideas" finds that exact phrase

Add specific details

Add words like "budget" or "small space" to focus results

Try different words

"Storage" might show different results than "organization"

Use helpful tools

YouTube, Pinterest, Reddit, Google, Perplexity, and other resources all offer different perspectives

HELPFUL ONLINE RESOURCES

YouTube

Great for seeing real people using products and explaining problems

Pinterest

Visual inspiration and creative solutions that people actually use

Blogs & Articles

Detailed reviews and advice from experts and other students

Google

Search for articles, reviews, and specific information

AI Search Tools

Tools like Perplexity that can summarize information from many sources

Surveys & Polls

Find statistics and research about what large groups of users prefer

MAIN POINTS

  • Look at reviews to find what people like and dislike
  • Visual examples show how people are really using products
  • Use many different online resources to get a complete picture
  • Better search terms lead to more useful results
