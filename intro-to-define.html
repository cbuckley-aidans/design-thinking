1

What Makes a Good Problem Statement?

The Problem Statement Formula

[USER] needs [NEED] because [REASON]
  • USER: Who exactly are we designing for?
  • NEED: What do they need to be able to do?
  • REASON: Why is this so important for them?

PS5 Example

Weak Problem Statement

"Gamers need better controllers."

Strong Problem Statement

"Gamers with physical challenges need a controller they can change to fit them, because standard controllers can be hard for them to use."

Why is the second one better? It's specific, focuses on the user, and explains the real issue!

2

Problems vs Solutions

Don't Jump to Solutions!

Focus on WHY

What's the real need or frustration?

Not WHAT

Don't describe the thing you want to build yet.

Good problems inspire lots of different creative solutions!

Step Inside Thinking Routine

Harvard Project Zero: "Step Inside"

Use these questions to really get to know your user's world.

What might this person be THINKING?

What are their worries, hopes, or questions? What's going on in their head?

What might this person be FEELING?

Are they frustrated, excited, anxious, or happy?

What might this person be EXPERIENCING?

What's their day actually like? What's happening around them?

What might this person REALLY NEED?

Look past the obvious. What deeper need would make things truly better for them?

Use what you've learned to write your problem statement on the worksheet.

3

Problem Statement Framework

Use this framework on your worksheet

[USER] needs [NEED] because [REASON]
[USER]: Be specific. Not just "people," but maybe "busy working parents" or "a teenager learning to cook."
[NEED]: What do they need to be able to do? Focus on the goal, not the solution.
[REASON]: Why does this matter? What did you learn from "stepping inside" their world?

Remember: A great problem can lead to many different, amazing solutions!

CHUNK 2: CREATING SUCCESS RULES

TEACHER CHUNK
13 mins

From a Problem to Clear Rules for Success

1

What are Success Rules (Design Criteria)?

Success Rules are a list of clear, testable standards your design must meet to be successful.

  • Specific: The rules are super clear, not vague.
  • Measurable: You can actually check if your design follows the rule.
  • User-focused: Based on what the user really needs.
  • Realistic: Possible to achieve.

The magic: They turn a fuzzy problem into a clear target to aim for!

Success Rule Examples

"It must..."

...be possible to set up in under 30 seconds.

...be usable by someone who can't use a keyboard.

...cost less than $50 to make.

2

PS5 Access Controller Rules Example

From Problem to Success Rules

Problem Statement:

"Gamers with physical challenges need a controller they can change to fit them, because standard controllers can be hard for them to use."

Sony's Success Rules might have been:

How it Works: Must plug in and work with a PS5 straight away.

How Easy it is to Use: Must let users change button layouts in less than 2 minutes.

Physical: Must be able to be held or mounted in at least 5 different ways.

How it makes people Feel: Must help gamers feel like part of the team, not left out.

3

Types of Success Rules

How it WORKS

What must the solution DO?

  • Speed, power
  • Works with other devices
  • Doesn't crash

How EASY it is to USE

How simple and clear is it?

  • Easy to learn
  • Hard to make mistakes
  • Works for everyone

PHYSICAL Rules

What are its physical limits?

  • Size and weight
  • Materials, strong/weak
  • Cost to make

How it makes people FEEL

What emotion should it create?

  • Makes them feel confident
  • Reduces stress
  • Makes them feel included

5

Creating Strong Rules

Ideas for Writing Great Success Rules

Think about all these different areas when you write your rules.

How it WORKS
Performance
  • "Must finish [task] in less than [X] seconds"
  • "Must work for [X] people at once"
  • "Must run for [X] hours without stopping"
Compatibility
  • "Must work with [phones/laptops/etc.]"
  • "Must connect using [Bluetooth/USB/etc.]"
Reliability
  • "Must not crash or freeze"
  • "Must survive being used [X] times"
  • "Must work in [hot/cold/wet] weather"
How EASY it is to USE
Easy to Learn
  • "Must be learnable in [X] minutes"
  • "Must take less than [X] steps to do a basic task"
Works for Everyone
  • "Must be usable by people with [e.g. vision problems]"
  • "Must have simple language"
Clear to Understand
  • "Must be understandable without instructions"
  • "Important info must be easy to spot"
PHYSICAL Rules
Size & Weight
  • "Must be smaller than [X] cm"
  • "Must weigh less than [X] grams"
  • "Must be easy to carry"
Toughness
  • "Must survive being dropped from [height]"
  • "Must last for [X] years"
  • "Must be waterproof"
Cost
  • "Must cost less than $[X] to make"
  • "Must be affordable for [the user]"
How it LOOKS & makes people FEEL
Looks
  • "Must look [modern/fun/professional]"
  • "Must appeal to [teenagers/kids/etc.]"
  • "Should look good for years"
Feelings
  • "Must make users feel more confident"
  • "Must reduce stress when doing [a task]"
  • "Must create a feeling of [e.g. fun]"
Connection
  • "Must help users feel included"
  • "Must help people connect with others"
Planet & Future-Friendly Rules
Planet-Friendly
  • "Must use recycled materials"
  • "Must be easy to recycle at the end of its life"
  • "Must use less energy"
New & Different
  • "Must do something no other product does"
  • "Must be a big improvement on old solutions"
Long-Lasting
  • "Must be useful for many years"
  • "Should be easy to fix, not just throw away"

Your Goal: Create 4-6 specific rules for your design, covering different types from above.

WRAP-UP: CHECKING OUR WORK & REFLECTING

1

Key Takeaways

What We've Learned Today

  • Looking at other designs helps us see what choices the designers made.
  • "Stepping Inside" our user's world helps us understand what they really need.
  • Good problem statements focus on the user's need, not the solution we want to build.
  • "Success Rules" turn a fuzzy problem into clear, testable targets.
  • Using different types of rules helps make sure we've thought of everything.

What's Next?

Now that we have a clear problem and our Success Rules, we're ready to:

  • Ideate: Brainstorm lots of creative ideas that follow our Success Rules.
  • Prototype: Build quick, simple models of our best ideas.
  • Test: Get feedback from users and use our Success Rules to check how well our solution works.

Real-World Connection

Just like the PS5 Access Controller was guided by clear rules like "must be mounted in 5 different ways" and "users must be able to change buttons in under 2 minutes," your designs will be much stronger when you have your own clear, testable Success Rules to guide you. Defining a good problem and setting clear rules are what professional designers do to create things that really help people.
