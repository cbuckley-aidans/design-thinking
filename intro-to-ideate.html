What is a Mood Board?

A mood board is a collection of visual elements that captures the look, feel, and style you want your design to have.

What Goes in a Mood Board:

  • Colours: Palettes that match your aesthetic
  • Textures: Materials and surface qualities
  • Typography: Font styles and text treatments
  • Shapes & Forms: Geometric or organic elements
  • Existing Products: Designs that inspire you
  • Lifestyle Images: How people use similar products

Mood boards help your whole team share the same vision!

IDEATION SKETCHING: THINK WITH YOUR HANDS

The goal is quantity and exploration, not perfect drawings!

1

Why Sketching Beats Pure Thinking

Thinking Only in Your Head

  • Ideas stay abstract and vague
  • Hard to share with others
  • Easy to get stuck on one idea
  • Difficult to see problems
  • Limited by working memory
ü§î ‚Üí üòï

Sketching While Thinking

  • Ideas become concrete and clear
  • Easy to communicate concepts
  • Generates many variations quickly
  • Reveals design problems early
  • External memory on paper
‚úèÔ∏è ‚Üí üí°üí°üí°

Professional designers sketch constantly - even for simple ideas!

2

The Three Rules of Ideation Sketching

‚ö°

1. Draw from Your Shoulder

Not your wrist! Use your whole arm for confident, flowing lines.

  • Hold pen/pencil loosely
  • Move your entire arm
  • Makes bold, confident strokes
  • Faster and more expressive
üî¢

2. Quantity Over Quality

Aim for 5+ sketches in a short session. More ideas = better final solutions.

  • No erasing allowed!
  • 30 seconds per sketch maximum
  • Focus on getting ideas out
  • Refine later, not now
üìè

3. Keep It Simple

Basic shapes and labels communicate ideas clearly without detail.

  • Use rectangles, circles, lines
  • Add simple labels for clarity
  • Show key features only
  • Save details for later
3

Sketching Techniques for Products

Show Different Views & Functions

Different Angles
  • Top view (layout and arrangement)
  • Side view (thickness, proportions)
  • User's perspective (in use)
  • Close-ups of key features
Show Functionality
  • How components interact
  • Movement and flexibility
  • Connection points
  • User interaction methods

Pro tip: Add arrows, labels, and simple annotations to make your ideas crystal clear!

Remember: You're designing ideas, not final technical drawings!

2

From Sketch to SCAMPER Innovation

Example: Basic Water Bottle Sketch ‚Üí SCAMPER Variations

Original Sketch Idea
üíß

Reusable water bottle with measurement lines

S - Substitute: What if we replaced the cap?
  • Magnetic closure instead of screw-on
  • Built-in straw instead of opening
  • Smart cap that tracks water intake
C - Combine: What if we merged it with other items?
  • Water bottle + phone charger
  • Bottle + Bluetooth speaker
  • Hydration + temperature control
E - Eliminate: What if we removed assumptions?
  • No rigid shape - collapsible design
  • Remove the need to refill manually
  • Eliminate plastic materials entirely
R - Reverse: What if we flipped the approach?
  • Water comes to you instead of carrying it
  • Hot drinks instead of cold water
  • Dispenses vitamins, not just water

SCAMPER transforms one simple idea into many innovative possibilities!

3

Making SCAMPER Work for Any Design

Focus on User Needs

Every SCAMPER question should consider:

  • Usability: Easier ways to interact
  • Functionality: Better performance
  • Experience: More enjoyable to use
  • Context: Different usage scenarios

SCAMPER for Innovation

Use SCAMPER to ask:

  • Substitute: Alternative materials or methods?
  • Modify: Different sizes, shapes, or features?
  • Eliminate: Remove unnecessary complexity?
  • Adapt: Learn from other industries?

The best design solutions often come from unexpected SCAMPER combinations!
