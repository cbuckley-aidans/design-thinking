What is Prototyping?

Prototyping gets ideas out of your head and into the world, allowing for quick learning and exploration.

Physical Form

A prototype can be anything tangible - from post-it notes to role-playing activities to physical objects.

Interactive Learning

Prototypes work best when people can experience and interact with them, starting conversations and driving empathy.

Purpose-Driven

Every prototype should have a clear learning goal - know what you're testing before you start building.

Improvising to Life

Break through "analysis paralysis" by quickly acting out your ideas.

Assign Roles

Designate team members as users, technology, and observers

Act It Out

Physically simulate the interaction to understand pain points

Iterate

Swap roles and try different approaches to test variations

Discuss

Break to analyze what's working and what needs improvement

User-Driven Prototyping

User-driven prototypes are created by users rather than developed by you, revealing assumptions and desires that otherwise remain unmined.

Purpose

Your goal is not to integrate their ideas into your design, but to better understand their thinking and reveal undetected needs and insights.

Balancing Structure

Strike a balance between how much you provide and how much you ask users to create. The ideal prototype should be scaffolded enough to spur generative thinking, but open enough to broaden user understanding.

Prototype Methods

  • Drawing tasks: Ask users to "draw going to the doctor"
  • Object creation: Have users "make a diaper bag with this paper and tape"
  • Artifact compilation: Ask users to "tear out magazine pictures that depict your ideal mall shopping experience"

Example Implementation

When designing a custom t-shirt website, consider giving users a blank page with empty boxes as scaffolding, letting them draw their ideal website layout rather than showing them a traditional mockup. This reveals their mental models and expectations.

Wizard of Oz Prototyping

A technique where humans secretly operate a seemingly automated system to test user reactions and interactions

The Technique

Named after the famous story where a wizard was actually a man behind a curtain, this method involves creating an illusion of functionality. Instead of building complex systems, human operators simulate the responses and actions, while users believe they're interacting with an automated system.

Physical Examples

  • A vending machine where items are secretly delivered by a person
  • An "automatic" door controlled by hidden staff
  • A "smart" storage system with manual sorting behind the scenes

Implementation

Create a convincing facade that users can interact with, while team members operate the system from behind the scenes. Focus on making the experience feel authentic to get genuine user feedback.

Testing Variables

Instead of building complete solutions, test one specific aspect at a time to learn faster and more effectively.

How to Choose Variables

  • Identify the most critical aspect to test
  • Focus on one property that could make or break the design
  • Choose variables that users can easily compare

Example Variables to Test

  • Weight: Different weighted prototypes to find ideal heaviness
  • Size: Various dimensions to determine perfect fit
  • Interface: Different button layouts or menu structures

Real-World Examples

Testing chair comfort: Create seat cushions of different firmness without building entire chairs
App navigation: Paper prototypes with different menu structures, testing only the navigation flow
Product packaging: Multiple box sizes with simple labels to test shelf appeal

Key Principle

Remember: A prototype doesn't need to look like the final product. Focus only on what you need to learn about the chosen variable.
