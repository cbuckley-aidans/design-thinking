Empathy / Empathise

Simple Definition

Being able to understand and share another person's feelings and needs.

Detailed Definition

Empathy means understanding how other people feel and what they need. When designers use empathy, they try to see the world through the eyes of the people who will use their product.

Why It's Important

  • Helps you understand what users really need, not just what they say they need
  • Helps find out what makes users frustrated or confused
  • Leads to better designs that people actually want to use

How to Use It

You can develop empathy for your users by:

  1. Roleplay: Watch people as they use a product to see where they get stuck
  2. Interview: Ask questions and listen carefully to understand what users want and why
  3. Research: Learn about users' needs through searching online

Example in Use:

"We need to empathise with the students who will use these lockers to understand how to improve them."

User Story

Simple Definition

A short description of something a user wants to do, written from their point of view.

Detailed Definition

User stories help you understand exactly what your users need. They are short statements that explain who the user is, what they want to do, and why they want to do it.

Format

User stories follow this pattern:

As a type of user, I want to do something so that I get this benefit.

  • WHO: The type of person who will use your design
  • WHAT: The action they want to take
  • WHY: The benefit they get

Example in Use:

"We created several user stories to better understand the needs of students with different learning styles."

Format Examples:

  1. "As a student who plays sports, I want to keep my gym clothes separate from my school books so that my books don't get smelly or damp."
  2. "As a student with ADHD, I want to break down big assignments into smaller steps so that I don't feel overwhelmed and can track my progress."

Persona Use case User requirements User needs

Design Criteria

Simple Definition

A list of specific requirements that your design must meet to be successful.

Detailed Definition

Design criteria are the rules that help you check if your design is good. They are like a checklist for your project that you can test at the end.

Creating Good Criteria

  1. Be Specific: Use clear numbers instead of vague words
    • Bad: "The water bottle must be light"
    • Good: "The water bottle must weigh less than 500g"
  2. Make it Testable: You need to be able to check if your design meets the criteria
    • Bad: "Must be strong"
    • Good: "Must survive a 1m drop test without breaking"
  3. Keep it Achievable: Make sure you can meet your criteria with the materials and skills you have
  4. Make it Relevant: Each criterion should solve a real problem for your users

Example in Use:

"The teacher asked us to create clear design criteria so we could properly test if our project was successful."

Example Set of Criteria:

  1. Leak-proof: The lunchbox must not leak when shaken upside down for 10 seconds.
  2. Insulated: Must keep food at least 22°C above room temperature for 4 hours.
  3. Lightweight: Must weigh less than 500g when empty.
  4. Durable: Must withstand being dropped from desk height (75cm) 10 times without cracking.

Prototype

Simple Definition

An early, simple model of a product that lets you test how it works before making the final version.

Detailed Definition

A prototype is a simple version of your idea that you can test before making the final product. Prototypes help you learn what works and what needs to be changed.

Types of Prototypes

  • Physical prototypes: Made from simple materials like paper, cardboard, clay, or recycled materials
  • Act it Out: Roleplaying how a product or service would work
  • Wizard of Oz: Making users think something works automatically when it's actually controlled by a person
  • Digital prototypes: Simple digital versions of apps or websites

Why We Make Prototypes

  • Test ideas quickly and cheaply before spending time and money
  • Get feedback from users early in the design process
  • Identify problems and make improvements
  • Try different options to find the best solution

Example in Use:

"Our team created three different prototypes to test which design worked best for the users."

Other Examples:

  • "We made a cardboard prototype of our water bottle design to test if the size felt comfortable to hold."
  • "The paper prototype of our app let us test if users could understand the navigation."

Related Terms:

Mock-up Model Wireframe Testing Iteration

SCAMPER

Simple Definition

A method for generating new ideas by changing parts of something that already exists. Each letter in SCAMPER stands for a different way to change your design.

Detailed Definition

SCAMPER is a creative thinking tool that helps you improve an existing design by asking specific questions that spark new ideas.

What Each Letter Means

  • S = Substitute: Replace parts of your design with something else

    Example: What if we used bamboo instead of plastic?

  • C = Combine: Mix different ideas or products together

    Example: What if we combined a phone with a projector?

  • A = Adapt: Adjust your design to solve different problems

    Example: How could this toy work for older kids?

  • M = Modify: Change the size, shape, or features

    Example: What if we made it bigger or smaller?

  • P = Put to another use: Find different ways to use your design

    Example: Could this school item be used at home?

  • E = Eliminate: Remove unnecessary parts

    Example: What features aren't really needed?

  • R = Reverse: Try turning your design around or upside down

    Example: What if we did this backwards?

Example in Use:

"During our class, we used the SCAMPER technique to come up with innovative improvements to our initial water bottle design."

How to Use SCAMPER:

  1. Start with an existing product or idea
  2. Go through each letter of SCAMPER and ask the related questions
  3. Write down all your new ideas
  4. Select the best ideas to develop further

Mood Board

Simple Definition

A group of pictures and colours arranged together to show the style or "mood" you want your design to have.

Detailed Definition

A mood board is a collection of images, colours, patterns, and other visual elements that show the style or "feeling" you want for your design.

What a Mood Board Includes

  • Images that inspire you
  • Colour schemes
  • Patterns and textures
  • Typography (font styles)
  • Materials you might use

Why Use Mood Boards

  • Helps you define the visual style of your project
  • Gets everyone on the same page about the design direction
  • Provides a source of inspiration when you're stuck
  • Helps make your design more consistent

Example in Use:

"Before starting our project, we created a mood board with images of sustainable packaging to inspire our eco-friendly container design."

Related Terms:

Inspiration board Design style Visual research Aesthetic Colour palette

Iteration

Simple Definition

The process of making a design better by testing it, making changes, and testing it again multiple times.

Detailed Definition

Iteration means making your design better through repeated cycles of testing and improvement. It's the process of making changes based on feedback and creating new versions of your design.

How Iteration Works

  1. Create a version of your design (like a prototype)
  2. Test it with users and collect feedback
  3. Identify things that need to be improved
  4. Make changes to your design
  5. Test the new version
  6. Repeat steps 2-5 until your design works well

Why Iteration Is Important

  • No design is perfect the first time
  • Each cycle makes your design better
  • Helps you learn from your mistakes
  • Reduces the risk of problems in the final design

Example in Use:

"The design process required several iterations before we arrived at a solution that met all our criteria."

Other Examples:

  • "After testing our first prototype, we went through three iterations to fix the issues users found."
  • "Each iteration of our app design made the navigation clearer for users."

Related Terms:

Feedback loop Refinement Versioning Continuous improvement

Sustainability

Simple Definition

Making design choices that are good for the environment and can continue for a long time without running out of resources.

Detailed Definition

Sustainability refers to creating designs that meet our needs today without harming the environment or using up resources that future generations will need.

Key Sustainability Factors

  • Production Impact: How much energy and resources are used to create the material
  • Transport Footprint: How far materials travel and the carbon footprint
  • Lifespan: How long products made from this material will last
  • End of Life: What happens to the material when it's no longer needed

Sustainable Design Strategies

  • Use renewable or recycled materials
  • Design for durability and long life
  • Minimise waste in production
  • Make products that can be repaired
  • Design for easy recycling or composting at end of life

Example in Use:

"Sustainability was a key consideration in our design process, leading us to choose bamboo instead of plastic for our product."

Eco-friendly Green design Circular economy Biodegradable Upcycling Renewable resources

Material Properties

Simple Definition

The qualities that describe what a material is like, such as how it looks, feels, and behaves.

Detailed Definition

Material properties are the specific characteristics of materials used in a design, such as how they look, feel, and perform. These properties help designers choose the right materials for their projects.

Main Material Properties

  • Look: Colour, transparency, finish
  • Feel: Texture, temperature, weight
  • Strength: How much force it can take
  • Flexibility: How much it can bend
  • Durability: How long it will last
  • Water Resistance: How it reacts to water

Example in Use:

"We needed to carefully consider the material properties of both acrylic and plywood before deciding which one to use for our laser-cut project."

Simple Comparison:

Property Acrylic Plywood
Look Clear or coloured, glossy Natural wood grain
Water Resistance Excellent Poor to moderate
Weight Lightweight Heavier than acrylic
